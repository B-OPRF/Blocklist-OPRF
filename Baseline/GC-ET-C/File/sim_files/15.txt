<heading id="h-0002" level="1">TECHNICAL FIELD</heading>
<p id="p-0003" num="0002">The present disclosure is generally related to towed implements, and, more particularly, tandem axles for towed agricultural implements.</p>
<heading id="h-0003" level="1">BACKGROUND</heading>
<p id="p-0004" num="0003">Towed implements, such as those with soil working implements (e.g., row units) attached, often use walking tandems. Walking tandems use wheels (including the tire mounted thereon) in a configuration in which one wheel of the tandem leads the other by a defined distance. However, since one wheel leads the other, a sharp turn may cause the lagging wheel to drag or push, creating a small mound of dirt that may compromise yield, particularly in no till conditions. Further, when space is a limitation to the rear, a center point (center of gravity) of the walking beam is shifted, which places more mass behind the tandem pivot, causing less weight to be carried on the hitch. This condition may lead to situations of negative tongue weight, which may cause a corresponding hitch to rise at the tail end of the towing vehicle. Traditional duals may be used, but one shortcoming to using them is that the load is not equalized over both wheels and spindles under certain conditions (e.g., when one of the wheels encounters an obstacle or trench or furrow), which may result in undesirable high compaction areas (e.g., for planting applications).</p>
<heading id="h-0004" level="1">BRIEF SUMMARY</heading>
<p id="p-0005" num="0004">In one embodiment, an in-line tandem axle assembly comprising: a tandem wheel arm; a pair of wheel connecting assemblies, each comprising a hub and a spindle; a pair of mounts coupled respectively to the pair of wheel connecting assemblies; a pair of wheels coupled respectively to the pair of wheel connecting assemblies, the pair of wheels separated by the tandem wheel arm and in a same fore-and-aft position; and plural pairs of parallel links of equal length pivotably mounted to the pair of mounts and pivotably mounted to the tandem wheel arm.</p>

<heading id="h-0006" level="1">DETAILED DESCRIPTION</heading>
<p id="p-0014" num="0013">Certain embodiments of an in-line tandem axle assembly, system, and method are disclosed that replace a walking beam tandem, such as used on conventional implements, with a set or sets of parallel links to enable the wheels (hereinafter, reference to wheel or wheels includes the tire(s) mounted thereon except where distinguished below) to be in a same fore-and-aft position while flexing independently to enable an equalization of ground pressure (e.g., loads) between the two wheels. Such a configuration of the disclosed embodiments also provides the space efficiency of dual wheels when compared to tandem walking beams while employing a similar load distribution effect.</p>
<p id="p-0015" num="0014">Digressing briefly, conventional implements often use walking beam tandem configurations to distribute the weight load. However, walking beam tandems, with their different fore-and-aft wheel placement, consume more space than in-line duals, and also tend to place more weight on the implement, sometimes resulting in what is often referred to as a negative tongue weight that can lift the back end of a towing vehicle under certain conditions. Also, the walking beam tandem configuration tends to create what is referred to as a scrubbing effect, where for tight turns, one of the tandem wheels drags, creating a mound of dirt that can compromise seeding depth and hence yield. In contrast, certain embodiments of an in-line tandem axle assembly provide a more space-efficient assembly while eliminating or mitigating the risk of scrubbing and negative tongue weights. For instance, since the in-line configuration enables the wheels to be positioned closer to a row unit than walking beam tandem configurations, more weight can be carried by a towing vehicle hitch to maintain a positive tongue weight.</p>

<p id="p-0018" num="0017">Referring now to <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>1</b></figref>, shown is an example implement <b>10</b>A in which an embodiment of an example in-line tandem axle assembly may be used. Although depicted using an AGCO White planter, it should be appreciated by one having ordinary skill in the art that other types of implements in the same or different industries (and/or by different manufacturers) that use tandem wheels may be used, and hence are contemplated to be within the scope of the disclosure. The implement <b>10</b>A is typically pulled by a towing vehicle, including a tractor or combine harvester. The implement <b>10</b>A comprises a hitch or tongue assembly <b>12</b>, which is coupled to the towing vehicle as is known. The implement <b>10</b>A comprises a center portion <b>14</b> and two wing portions <b>16</b>A, <b>16</b>B, which are pivotably coupled to the center portion <b>14</b>. For instance, the wing portions <b>16</b>A, <b>16</b>B may be folded to enable a narrow profile for road transport and/or storage. The center portion <b>14</b> comprises an underlying frame that supports product and components enabling the delivery of product to field working components attached thereto. The center portion <b>14</b> is supported by plural wheels <b>18</b>, <b>20</b> (e.g., <b>18</b>A, <b>18</b>B, <b>20</b>A, <b>20</b>B) configured in one embodiment with an in-line tandem axle assembly. The wheels <b>18</b>A, <b>18</b>B comprise one set of in-line wheels and the wheels <b>20</b>A, <b>20</b>B comprise the opposing set of in-line wheels. The wing portions <b>16</b>A and <b>16</b>B comprise respective single wheels <b>22</b> and <b>24</b> supporting the wing portions <b>16</b>A, <b>16</b>B.</p>
<p id="p-0019" num="0018"><figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>2</b></figref> is a schematic diagram that illustrates, in fragmentary, overhead plan view, another example implement <b>10</b>B, which may be similarly configured to that shown in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>1</b></figref> or in some embodiments, configured according to a different design. The implement <b>10</b>B comprises a hitch/tongue assembly <b>26</b> that is typically coupled to a towing vehicle for field operations. The implement <b>10</b>B comprises a center portion <b>28</b> (partially shown) and a wing portion <b>30</b> (as well as a mirrored wing portion not shown in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>2</b></figref>) that is pivotably coupled to the center portion <b>28</b>. The center portion <b>28</b> comprises a supporting frame that includes a transverse extending (transverse to forward motion of the implement <b>10</b>B) bar <b>32</b> from which a tandem wheel arm (best shown in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>3</b>A</figref>) is coupled and extends downward (e.g., orthogonal to the bar <b>32</b>) to a pair of in-line, tandem wheels <b>34</b> (e.g., <b>34</b>A, <b>34</b>B) (that is also mirrored on the other side of the center portion <b>28</b> but omitted from the depicted view). The center portion <b>28</b> further comprises a tool bar <b>36</b>. The tool bar <b>36</b> is parallel to, and rearward of, the bar <b>32</b>. The pair of in-line tandem wheels <b>34</b> (and opposing pair not shown) are forward of the tool bar <b>36</b>. Coupled to the tool bar <b>36</b> are plural row units <b>38</b>. In one embodiment, the row units <b>38</b> are staggered fore and aft, with some of the row units <b>38</b> closer to the bar <b>32</b> in fore and aft direction and some further from the bar <b>32</b>. A subset of the row units <b>38</b> coupled to the tool bar <b>36</b> are partially disposed between the wheels <b>34</b>A and <b>34</b>B (e.g., surrounded on two sides by the interior sides of the wheels <b>34</b>A, <b>34</b>B), which illustrates the importance of maintaining a wide enough gap between the wheels <b>34</b>A, <b>34</b>B and also maintaining the camber (e.g., the distance between the longitudinal center lines of each wheel <b>34</b>A, <b>34</b>B) at a constant or substantially constant dimension (e.g., the length between) to avoid interference with the protruding portion of the row unit <b>38</b>. In one embodiment, the pair of wheels <b>34</b> may be angled at zero degrees (e.g., pointing directly forward), and in some embodiments, there may be a slight angle imposed equally to the pair of wheels <b>34</b>. The wing portion <b>30</b> comprises, fore and aft, a draw bar <b>40</b> and tool bar <b>42</b> that are oriented in parallel with one another, the tool bar <b>42</b> pivotably coupled to the draw bar <b>40</b> via plural arms <b>43</b> that extend from the bars <b>40</b>, <b>42</b> and hingeably couple therebetween.</p>
<p id="p-0020" num="0019">Having described example implements <b>10</b> (e.g., <b>10</b>A, <b>10</b>B) in which an embodiment of an in-line tandem axle assembly may be used, attention is directed to <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIGS. <b>3</b>A-<b>3</b>B</figref>, which illustrate one embodiment of an example in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A. The in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A illustrates one of the assemblies for one of the pairs of in-line wheels for a center portion of an implement, where another similarly-structured assembly is duplicated for another pair of wheels. For instance, each in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A is used for each pair of wheels <b>18</b> (e.g., <b>18</b>A, <b>18</b>B), <b>20</b> (e.g., <b>20</b>A, <b>20</b>B) shown in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>1</b></figref>. Referring in particular to <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>3</b>A</figref>, an embodiment of the in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A comprises a tandem wheel arm (or for brevity, simply referred to as an arm) <b>46</b>, parallel links <b>48</b> (pairs of parallel links comprising upper <b>48</b>A and lower <b>48</b>B links, with the understanding that another pair of upper and lower links is opposite the arm <b>46</b> as best shown in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>5</b></figref>), a pair of mounts <b>50</b> (e.g., <b>50</b>A, <b>50</b>B), and a pair of wheels <b>52</b> (e.g., <b>52</b>A, <b>52</b>B). It should be appreciated that in some embodiments, the in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A may include fewer or additional components. The arm <b>46</b> is coupled to a transverse extending bar, such as the bar <b>32</b> depicted in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>2</b></figref>. In one embodiment, the arm <b>46</b> may be welded to the bar <b>32</b>, or secured by other securement mechanisms (e.g., bolted, formed via a casting or forging process, etc.). The arm <b>46</b> extends downward from the bar <b>32</b>, and extends between the wheels <b>52</b> to a location proximal to the lowest flex point for the wheel spindle, as described further below.</p>
<p id="p-0021" num="0020">The parallel links <b>48</b> are pivotably coupled to the arm <b>46</b> at relative upper and lower locations for respective central cavities (not shown) to enable independent flexing of one wheel <b>52</b> (e.g., <b>52</b>A or <b>52</b>B) relative to the other wheel <b>52</b> (e.g., <b>52</b>B or <b>52</b>A). By enabling independent flexing, one or more benefits may be realized, including equal load distribution (which also has the corollary benefit of enabling the tire specification (e.g., maximum inflation pressure specification) to be reduced since the assumption is no longer that all of the load will be borne by one wheel, as is typically the case with in-line or dual wheel designs). The parallel links <b>48</b> are also pivotably coupled to the mounts <b>50</b> for each wheel <b>52</b>, as described in further detail in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>5</b></figref>. Note that reference to wheels <b>52</b> includes the respective tires mounted thereon, as well as the inner rims and associated components where not explicitly referenced. The parallel links <b>48</b> (which in one embodiment comprise plural pairs of parallel links, including an upper pair and lower pair on one side of the arm <b>46</b> and another upper and lower pair on the opposite side of the arm <b>46</b>) enable the wheels <b>52</b> to flex up and down (e.g., to different elevations), such as when encountering an obstacle or surface unevenness (e.g., including ditch, mound, etc.) in the field. By enabling the flex motion, the in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A achieves the wheel motion independence and load distribution benefits of a walking beam tandem, yet in a reduced space and improved (e.g., center of gravity) location. The parallel links <b>48</b>A, <b>48</b>B are of equal size (including the upper and lower pair duplicated aft of the depicted links), and in combination with their pivotable coupling to the mounts <b>50</b> and arm <b>46</b>, enable the gap between the inner sides of the wheels <b>52</b> to remain substantially constant in dimension (e.g., transverse length), whether at a zero angle (both wheels aligned in a forward direction) or both slightly and equally angled. In other words, the camber between the wheels <b>52</b> is substantially constant. The importance of these features can be seen in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIGS. <b>3</b>A and <b>4</b></figref>, in which a row unit <b>57</b> secured to, for instance, a tool bar <b>56</b> (e.g., <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>3</b>A</figref>, and also shown in <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>2</b></figref>) encroaches at least partially within the space between the wheels <b>52</b>. A variable camber or insufficient length of gap between the wheels <b>52</b> may interfere with the row unit <b>57</b>. A further benefit of the in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A over walking beam tandem configurations, as explained above, is that the proximity of the row unit <b>57</b> to the wheels <b>52</b> enables the center of mass to be proximal to the wheels <b>52</b>, enabling a positive tongue weight during field operations.</p>
<p id="p-0022" num="0021">Referring to <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>3</b>B</figref>, shown is the in-line tandem axle assembly <b>44</b>A of <figref idref="DRAWINGS">FIG. <b>3</b>A and <b>4</b></figref> in further detail and for a level surface (no flexing of the wheels <b>52</b>). Shown are the previously enumerated component parts including the arm <b>46</b>, parallel links <b>48</b>, mounts <b>50</b>, and wheels <b>52</b>. The parallel links <b>48</b>A, <b>48</b>B are horizontally oriented, owing to the level surface upon which the wheels <b>52</b> are placed. The parallel links <b>48</b> are pivotably coupled to the arm <b>46</b> and the mounts <b>50</b>. The mounts <b>50</b> are each coupled (e.g., bolted, screwed, etc.) to a respective spindle <b>58</b> (e.g., <b>58</b>A, <b>58</b>B) extending inward from a respective hub, or generally, hub assembly <b>60</b> (e.g., <b>60</b>A, <b>60</b>B). As is known, each spindle <b>58</b> is part of a hub assembly <b>60</b> (e.g., <b>60</b>A, <b>60</b>B) that is mounted to the wheel <b>52</b> (e.g., bolted to the inner rim).</p>

Note that the inner rims <b>62</b>, spindles <b>58</b>, and hub <b>60</b> are collectively referred to herein as wheel connecting assemblies.</p>
